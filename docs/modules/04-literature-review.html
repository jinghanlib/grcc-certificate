Module 4: Literature Review Strategies

Design effective search strategies and navigate academic databases

🎯 Learning Objectives

After completing this module, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish between different types of literature reviews
  • Design an effective, reproducible search strategy
  • Use UC Library Search and discipline-specific databases effectively
  • Write an annotated bibliography
  • Select the most appropriate type of review for your research question

Overview

This module introduces the different types of reviews you may encounter in academic research. It guides you through designing an effective literature review search strategy and highlights UC Riverside Library resources that support you throughout the process. In addition to going through this page, review the tutorials and guides the at the Resources Section.

Types of Reviews

Different research questions call for different review methodologies. Understanding which type fits your project is essential before you begin searching. The descriptions below are drawn from the Baylor University Evidence Synthesis guide on Types of Reviews.

Literature (Narrative) Review

A broad term referring to reviews with a wide scope and non-standardized methodology.

  • Search strategies, comprehensiveness, and time range covered vary and do not follow an established protocol.

Rapid Review

Applies systematic review methodology within a time-constrained setting.

  • Employs methodological “shortcuts” (limiting search terms, for example) at the risk of introducing bias.
  • Useful for addressing issues needing quick decisions.

Scoping Review or Systematic Map

Systematically and transparently collects and categorizes existing evidence on a broad topic or set of research questions.

  • Seeks to identify research gaps and opportunities for evidence synthesis.
  • May critically evaluate existing evidence, but does not attempt to synthesize the results in the way a systematic review would.
  • May take longer than a systematic review.

Umbrella Review

Reviews other systematic reviews on a topic.

  • Often defines a broader question than is typical of a traditional systematic review.
  • Most useful when there are competing interventions to consider.

Systematic Review

A methodical and comprehensive literature synthesis focused on a well-formulated research question.

  • Aims to identify and synthesize all of the scholarly research on a particular topic, including both published and unpublished studies.
  • Conducted in an unbiased, reproducible way to provide evidence for practice and policy-making and to identify gaps in research.
  • May involve a meta-analysis.
  • Much more time-intensive than traditional literature reviews.

Meta-Analysis

A statistical technique for combining the findings from disparate quantitative studies.

  • Uses statistical methods to objectively evaluate, synthesize, and summarize results.
  • May be conducted independently or as part of a systematic review.

Comparison at a Glance

Review Type Scope Methodology Typical Use
Literature (Narrative) Review Wide Non-standardized Background chapters, introductions
Rapid Review Selective Abbreviated systematic Time-sensitive decisions
Scoping Review / Systematic Map Broad Systematic, no synthesis Mapping gaps, feasibility
Umbrella Review Broad Reviews of reviews Competing interventions
Systematic Review Exhaustive Rigorous, reproducible Practice, policy, research gaps
Meta-Analysis Selected studies Statistical combination Estimating effect sizes
Tip

Not sure which type fits your research? Consult your subject librarian or your advisor — they can help you match your research question to the appropriate methodology.

Designing Your Search Strategy

A good search strategy is systematic, reproducible, and documented. Follow these steps:

Step 1: Define Your Research Question

Use a framework like PICO (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome) for clinical research, or PCC (Population, Concept, Context) for scoping reviews.

Step 2: Identify Key Concepts

Break your question into 2–4 core concepts. For each concept, brainstorm:

  • Synonyms and alternate spellings
  • Broader and narrower terms
  • Abbreviations and acronyms

Step 3: Choose Your Databases

Start with UC Library Search and Google Scholar (Google Scholar tends to include everything the good, bad and everythin in between, so better to use it critically)! You can also use databases appropriate to your discipline. Explore databases specific to your discipline that UCR Library has access to at the UCR Library A-Z Databases

Discipline Recommended Databases
Sciences & Medicine PubMed, Web of Science
Social Sciences and Humanities JSTOR, Web of Science (Social Science Citation Index and Policy Citation Index), Academic Search Complete
Engineering arXiv, IEEE Xplore
All disciplines UC Library Search, Google Scholar

Step 4: Use Boolean Operators

Combine search terms with:

  • AND — narrows results (all terms must appear)
  • OR — broadens results (any term can appear)
  • NOT — excludes terms
  • Quotation marks — exact phrase search
  • Asterisk * — truncation (e.g., data* finds data, dataset, databases)

Step 5: Document Everything

Record every search: database, date, terms used, and number of results. This is required for systematic and scoping reviews, and good practice for any review.

Writing an Annotated Bibliography

An annotated bibliography combines a citation with a brief summary and evaluation of each source. It is useful for:

  • Tracking what you’ve read and why it’s relevant
  • Preparing for the literature review chapter of your thesis or dissertation
  • Demonstrating research depth in grant proposals

What Each Annotation Includes

  1. Full citation (in your discipline’s style)
  2. Summary of the work’s main argument or findings (2–3 sentences)
  3. Evaluation of its relevance, methodology, or limitations (1–2 sentences)
