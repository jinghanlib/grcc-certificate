What Is a Research Plan?

A research plan is a structured document that maps out your research goals, methods, and resources. It serves multiple purposes:

  • For you: Clarifies your thinking, identifies gaps, and keeps your project on track
  • For your committee: Shows your readiness to conduct independent research
  • For grant applications: Many funders require a research plan or project narrative
  • For your career: Demonstrates scholarly organization and planning skills

Key Components of a Graduate Research Plan

A strong research plan typically includes:

1. Research Overview

  • Your research question or thesis statement
  • Significance and broader impact of your work
  • How it fits within your field’s existing literature

2. Literature Review Strategy

  • Databases and search terms you will use
  • Type of review (systematic, narrative, scoping, etc.)
  • Timeline for completing your literature review

3. Methodology

  • Data collection methods (surveys, experiments, archival research, interviews, etc.)
  • Data analysis approach
  • Ethical considerations (IRB status if applicable)

4. Data Management

  • Types of data you will generate or collect
  • File organization and naming conventions
  • Storage, backup, and sharing plan
  • Data Management Plan (DMP) status

5. Citation and Reference Management

  • Citation management tool you are using
  • Citation style for your discipline

6. Publication and Dissemination Plan

  • Target journals or venues for publishing your research
  • Plans for sharing datasets or supplementary materials
  • Open access strategy
  • ORCID iD integration

7. Timeline

  • Key milestones (proposal defense, IRB approval, data collection, writing, submission)
  • Estimated completion dates

Writing Tips

TipYour Research Plan Is a Living Document

It will evolve as your research progresses. What matters most is showing that you’ve thought carefully about each component.

  • Be specific: Vague goals are hard to execute. “I will search PubMed and Scopus using the terms X, Y, Z” is better than “I will do a literature review.”
  • Be realistic: Your timeline should account for learning curves, advisor feedback, and unexpected delays.
  • Connect the parts: Show how your citation management, data management, and dissemination plans support each other.
  • Revise based on feedback: Share drafts with your advisor, subject librarian, and peers.
